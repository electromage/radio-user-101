# Radio Communications 101

A quick-start guide to two-way radio etiquette and basic procedures.

---

## The Basics

- **What is Push-to-Talk (PTT)?** This is the button which causes your radio to begin sending a signal for all other radios to hear. This is also called the "transmit" button.
- **Radio is one at a time.** Your radio only allows one person to speak at a time. If somebody else is still talking on the radio, they can not hear you.
- **What is a Hot Mic?** When a person accidentally holds their push-to-talk button without talking, or the microphone is accidentally pushed when it is in a pocket or backpack, we call this a hot mic (microphone). This is a bad situation because nobody else can use the radios to talk when there is a hot mic!

---

## Core Principles

- **Think before you transmit.** Know what you want to say before you press the push-to-talk button.
- **Be brief, be clear, be done.** Remember nobody else can speak when you do - keep messages short.
- **Listen first.** Never transmit over someone else. Before transmitting, listen for a few seconds to make sure the frequency is clear. Some radios will beep if you try to transmit when somebody else is talking.

---

## How to Use the Radio

1. **Hold the radio straight up and down** (vertical). Tilting it reduces antenna efficiency and weakens your signal.
1. **Keep the radio away from large metal objects** — vehicles, metal fences, machinery, etc. Metal close to the antenna reflects and absorbs your signal, cutting your range.

### How to transmit and begin talking

1. **Press and hold** the Push-to-Talk (PTT) button before speaking.
1. **Pause a beat** after pressing PTT before you start talking — the first fraction of a second can get clipped.
1. **Speak clearly** at a normal pace across the face of the microphone (not directly into it).
1. **Release PTT** when you're done speaking.

## How To Call Another Station

Use this format:

> **[Who you're calling], this is [who you are]. Over.**

An Example:

You are Medic 4 and you want to call Medic 1. 

> "Medic 1, this is Medic 4. Over."

The called station, Medic 1, then responds:

> "Medic 4, this is Medic 1. Go ahead."

If no one responds, wait 10–15 seconds and try again.

## Acknowledging and Responding

Some words, such as yes and no, are harder to understand over a radio. Instead we use shared words which are easier to understand.

| You want to...            | Say                          |
| ------------------------- | ---------------------------- |
| Confirm you received      | "Copy" or "Roger"            |
| Say yes                   | "Affirmative"                |
| Say no                    | "Negative"                   |
| Ask them to repeat        | "Say again"                  |
| Signal you're done talking for the moment | "Over"        |
| Signal the conversation is finished       | "Out" or "Clear" |

**Never say "Over and out."** "Over" means you expect a reply; "Out" means you don't. Pick only one.

## Giving a Brief Report (SALUTE Format)

When reporting what you observe, the SALUTE format keeps it organized:

| Letter | Stands for | Example                        |
| ------ | ---------- | ------------------------------ |
| S      | Size       | "Three vehicles"               |
| A      | Activity   | "Traveling at high speed"     |
| L      | Location   | "Moving east on Highway 9"       |
| U      | Unit/ID    | "Marked with blue logos"       |
| T      | Time       | "Observed at 1430"             |
| E      | Equipment  | "Two sedans, one pickup truck" |

Put it together on the radio:

> "Dispatch, this is Deesc 4. I have a report, over."
> *(wait for acknowledgment)*
> "Three vehicles, traveling at high speed, moving east on Highway 9, marked with blue logos, observed at 1430, two sedans and one pickup. Over."

---

## Giving Your Position

Use whatever reference system your group has agreed on (grid coordinates, landmarks, mile markers). Always confirm the format so there's no confusion.

> "Medic 4 is at the junction of Main Street and 5th Avenue."

---

## On-Air Etiquette

- **Don't use names.** Use callsigns or unit designators.
- **Don't chitchat.** Save casual conversation for face-to-face.
- **Don't swear or use profanity.** On many frequencies it's illegal; on all of them it's unprofessional.
- **Don't interrupt.** If someone is mid-transmission (still talking), wait for "Over" before you key up. If it's urgent, say "Break, break" to signal priority traffic.
- **Identify yourself** every time you transmit. Don't assume people recognize your voice.
- **Use plain language.** Avoid jargon or slang that others might not know. Say "I understand" instead of obscure codes.
- **Emergency traffic takes priority.** If you hear "Mayday" or "Emergency," clear the channel immediately.

---

## Emergency Calls

Prefix with the urgency level:

| Prefix               | Meaning                             |
| -------------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| **"Mayday, Mayday, Mayday"** | Life-threatening emergency  |
| **"Break, break"**   | Priority message, let me talk immediately |

Format:

> "Mayday, Mayday, Mayday. This is Deesc 4. We have a medical emergency at Main Street and 5th Avenue. One person injured, requesting immediate assistance and ambulance. Over."

---

## Radio Check

To test whether your radio is working:

> "Any station, this is Medic 4. Radio check, over."

Respond with signal quality:

| Response              | Meaning                  |
| --------------------- | ------------------------ |
| "Loud and clear"      | Strong signal, clear audio |
| "Readable but weak"   | Can understand, low signal |
| "Unreadable"          | Cannot understand        |

> "Medic 4, this is Medic 1, you are Loud and Clear. Out."

**Remember:** Good radio discipline keeps the channel clear, gets information across fast, and can save lives in an emergency.

**Don't worry if you can't remember everything.** Review these notes, but do not panic if you forget some detail. Stay calm, use plain language, be patient, and you will be ok.
